大明一统志

Da Ming Yi Tong Zhi
A Record of Ming Dynasty Geography and Customs
Authentic Reprint Available on Library of Congress

About the Manuscript

Da Ming Yi Tong Zhi (大明一统志 or 明一统志), or Survey of the Great Ming Empire, is a comprehensive record of geography and customs in Great Ming Empire. Li Xian (李贤) was the chief editor. The series was completed in 1461, or Tianshun 5.
Every volume of the book covers different geographic regions. For example, this is Volume 90 of the manuscript, which focuses on minor and foreign states such as An Nan, Zhan Cheng, and Xian Luo. What makes this item stand out is its neat formatting, detailed explanations, and the inclusion of maps to illustrate geographical locations regarding the content in the book.
The book is known for its fine details about each region: it details the empire's geography, history, custom, and products, while it is also known for omitting details on population, taxes, and government structure. The book, nonetheless, is very great for learning about the human geography of the Ming Dynasty.

The Author

The book was written by a group of authors led by Li Xian (李贤) and originally created in 1461. The creators, who were highly knowledgeable about the geography of their era, were commissioned by the government. Historically, the Hong Wu Emperor originally commanded six writers to create and publish this work as part of an official mission.
In the 1450s, there was already an imperial geography manuscript compiled for the Ming Dynasty called 寰宇通志, but Emperor Yingzong (明英宗) was not content with the work and commissioned the new team to craft 大明一统志. It is modeled after 大元大一统志, a similar work from the Yuan Dynasty, but it is more comprehensive and detailed than its predecessor. The book was not created in response to a single event, but rather as part of a long-term governmental project to document the empire's geography and customs comprehensively.

Contents & Chapters

The creators aimed to produce a complete and comprehensive reference series detailing geography and customs of the Ming Empire. Books in the collection focus on regions within Ming China itself as well as the foreign states. The pages are rich with unique seals, stamps, and extensive annotations that highlight the effort and engagement of previous readers.

Method of Preservation

Taking Vol.90 as an example. This particular copy is identified as a reprint, or recarve, of the original 1461 version that is redistributed. We can infer it is a reprint because some of the printed characters appear fairly blurry. Physically, the book is neatly bound with thread in a plain, undecorated style, which likely reflects the original binding method due to its close resemblance to more modern bookbinding.

Historical Timeline

The text is a Ming Dynasty geographical reference book that drew heavy inspiration from similar works of the Yuan Dynasty. It was not created in response to a single event, but rather as part of a long-term governmental project. While published for official purposes, copies likely reached enthusiasts and collectors as well. Today, it resides in the U.S. Library of Congress as part of the Chinese Rare Book Collection, having been selected from 5,300 valuable titles housed in their Asian Division.

Cultural Significance

This manuscript is a highly significant primary document from the Ming Dynasty documenting the geography and culture of various states. The current collection of Vol.90 reveals a strong collector's bias, evidenced by an inscription that reads: "賣衣買書志亦迂，愛護不異隨侯珠，有假不返遭神誅，子孫鬻之何其愚" (Selling clothes to buy books is eccentric; protecting them like the Pearl of Marquis Sui. If borrowed and not returned, may the gods strike you; if descendants sell this, how foolish they are). Analyzing this source offers a fascinating connection to the past, as some of the foreign states mentioned, like Xian Luo, are still recognizable names today. They are among some of the best documents available to learn about Ming history and geography.
